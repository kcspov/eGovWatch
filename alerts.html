Reporting

How to Report Identity Theft or Hacking

Reporting Identity Theft

If you suspect your personal information has been stolen or misused, follow these steps immediately:

  1. Secure your accounts -- Change all passwords for compromised accounts immediately. Enable two-factor authentication where available.
  2. Document everything -- Take screenshots of suspicious activity, save emails, and record all unauthorized transactions.
  3. Report to the NBI Cybercrime Division -- File a complaint at the NBI headquarters or through their online portal. Bring all documentation and a valid government ID.
  4. File a complaint with the NPC -- If your personal data was breached, file a complaint at privacy.gov.ph for investigation under the Data Privacy Act.
  5. Notify your bank -- Immediately inform your bank or financial institution if financial accounts are affected. Request a temporary freeze on compromised accounts.
  6. File a police report -- Visit your local police station cybercrime unit to file an official blotter report for documentation purposes.
Emergency Contacts:
NBI Cybercrime Division: (02) 8523-8231 ext. 3455
PNP Anti-Cybercrime Group: (02) 8723-0401 local 7483

Reporting Hacking Incidents

If your device, account, or system has been hacked, take these immediate actions:

  1. Disconnect from the internet -- If you suspect your device is compromised, disconnect immediately to prevent further data exfiltration.
  2. Do not delete evidence -- Preserve all logs, emails, and system states. Digital forensics will require this information.
  3. Report to DICT-CIRT -- The Cybersecurity Incident Response Team (CIRT) handles cybersecurity incidents. Email: cirt@dict.gov.ph
  4. File a complaint with DOJ-OOC -- The Office of Cybercrime can coordinate international cooperation if the hacking has cross-border elements.
  5. Run a full security scan -- Use reputable antivirus software to scan for malware, keyloggers, and other threats on your device.
  6. Update all credentials -- Change passwords for all accounts, starting with email and financial services. Use strong, unique passwords for each.
Emergency Contacts:
DICT-CIRT: cirt@dict.gov.ph
DOJ-OOC: cybercrime@doj.gov.ph

Detection

How to Detect Fake Websites and Investment Scams

Detecting Fake Websites

Protect yourself by watching for these warning signs of fraudulent websites:

  • Check the URL carefully -- Look for misspellings, extra characters, or unusual domain extensions (e.g., .gov.ph.xyz instead of .gov.ph)
  • Look for HTTPS and the padlock icon -- Legitimate sites use secure connections. However, note that some fake sites also use HTTPS.
  • Examine the design quality -- Fake sites often have poor formatting, broken images, grammar errors, and inconsistent branding.
  • Verify contact information -- Legitimate government sites display verifiable contact details. Call the official number to confirm.
  • Be cautious of urgency -- Fake sites often pressure visitors with countdown timers or threats of account suspension.
  • Check the WHOIS record -- Use domain lookup tools to verify the site's registration date and owner information.

Detecting Investment Scams

Watch for these red flags before investing in any opportunity:

  • Guaranteed high returns with no risk -- All investments carry risk. Promises of guaranteed returns are a major red flag.
  • Pressure to invest immediately -- Scammers create artificial urgency. Legitimate investments allow time for due diligence.
  • Unregistered with the SEC -- Always verify that the company and its investment products are registered with the Securities and Exchange Commission.
  • Complex or vague explanations -- If the investment strategy cannot be clearly explained, it may be fraudulent.
  • Recruitment-based earnings -- Ponzi schemes rely on recruiting new investors to pay existing ones. This is unsustainable.
  • No physical office or verifiable team -- Legitimate investment companies have registered offices and identifiable management teams.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions About Cybersecurity

Republic Act No. 10175, also known as the Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012, is the primary legislation in the Philippines that defines and penalizes cybercrimes. It covers offenses such as illegal access, data interference, system interference, misuse of devices, cybersquatting, computer-related fraud, computer-related identity theft, cybersex, child pornography, and libel committed through a computer system. The law also establishes the Office of Cybercrime under the DOJ and designates the NBI and PNP as the law enforcement agencies responsible for investigation.

Republic Act No. 10173, the Data Privacy Act of 2012, protects individual personal information in information and communications systems in the government and private sector. It ensures that personal data is processed fairly and lawfully, collected for specified purposes, kept accurate and up to date, and retained only as long as necessary. The National Privacy Commission (NPC) enforces this law. Any citizen whose data privacy rights have been violated can file a complaint with the NPC.

Official Philippine government websites use the .gov.ph domain extension. Always verify the complete URL before entering any personal information. You can also check the DICT's official directory of government websites. Be wary of sites that mimic government branding but use different domain extensions such as .com, .net, or .org. When in doubt, contact the agency directly through their official phone number to verify.

Do not click on any links or download attachments from suspicious messages. Do not reply or provide personal information. Block the sender and report the message. For suspicious text messages, you can report them to your mobile service provider. For phishing emails, forward them to the DICT-CIRT at cirt@dict.gov.ph. Take a screenshot of the message for documentation before deleting it. If you already clicked a link or provided information, immediately change your passwords and monitor your accounts for unusual activity.

You can report online scams to the following agencies: (1) NBI Cybercrime Division at (02) 8523-8231 ext. 3455 or through their website, (2) PNP Anti-Cybercrime Group at (02) 8723-0401 local 7483, (3) DOJ Office of Cybercrime at cybercrime@doj.gov.ph, and (4) DICT through their cybersecurity hotline. Prepare documentation including screenshots, transaction records, and any communication with the scammer. File a formal complaint at the nearest NBI or PNP office for official investigation.
